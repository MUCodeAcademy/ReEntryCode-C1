# Software Development Jobs

Some places you can look for jobs:

- linkedin.com
- indeed.com
- monster.com
- ziprecruiter.com
- Often just Googling for software developer jobs can be useful

After taking this class, most job titles that you can look for are:

- Web developer
- Front-end/Back-end developer
- Data analyst
- DevOps (testing)

Most of the time you'd want to look for "entry level" or "intern" for your first job. Sometimes they don't specify, however, and you have to look at the "years of experience" section for the posting.

Let's look at some on LinkedIn.

Some things to note about developer jobs:

- If they specify 1-2 years of experience, you should apply anyway. I would also count this class a a year of experience.
- Right now, it's a bit difficult to find a job. This wasn't always the case, and hopefully it will get better in the near future, but reaching out to a recruiter helps quite a bit. A lot of jobs are not actually publicly posted, and instead the companies ask recruiters to find people for them.
- The average salary for an entry level web developer is $62,000 in Omaha, Nebraska.
- You can also be a freelance web developer where individual clients will reach out to you and pay you a set fee for making them a website. However, it is important to have a good portfolio showcasing your skills if you want people to hire you.

# Preparing for a software developer job

Typically, there will be at least 2 interviews: the first will be a meeting with the hiring manager and will be a general "about you" interview. The second interview will typically be a coding interview, where a developer will give you a programming question and give you about 30 minutes to solve it. There could be more interviews, like one with HR or sometimes you will meet the entire developer team and interview with them.

Some things to note about the coding interview:

- To prepare, you can do some practice problems either with our classwork or on LeetCode: https://leetcode.com
- These problems they ask in interviews are usually not too difficult (unless you're applying at Google or something).
- The most important thing is to explain your thought process during the interview. It doesn't matter too much whether you solve it correctly or not - the interviewer is just trying to see if you can think through a problem and explain what you're doing.
- There's a book in the BookRecommendations.md file called "Cracking the Coding Interview" which is very popular and helpful for the coding interviews specifically.
